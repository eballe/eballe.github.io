In the last three weeks my19-year-old daughter has moved in with her boyfriend and his parents. It started with her sleeping over there, then one night and I told her that I do not like or want her doing this, and the nights increased to three nights a week. I keep telling her I am not happy with this. Instead of changing, now she has been staying there for three weeks. We talk everyday, I keep telling her I love her and want her home. She has been home for one night and brief visits. I have repeatedly spoken to the boy's parents and it seems they are encouraging this. My heart is breaking daily. What can I do?

— Jazzy

 

Dear Jazzy,

Since your daughter is an adult, you have limited say over her decisions. However, if your daughter's boyfriend is not treating her with respect or making unhealthy decisions, then you may have to intervene in a more forceful way. If that is not the case, the best advice I can give you is to make sure you keep talking to her. But don't keep reminding her that you want her home. Instead, tell her how much you appreciate the phone calls and the short visits home. When she does visit, no matter how brief that is, let her know that you're very happy to see her and try to plan a fun activity with her. Your daughter may need to be reminded about the positive aspects of a loving mother-daughter relationship. Basically, I'm suggesting that you cut down on the nagging, no matter how upset you are, and increase your acceptance of her choices.

— January 6, 2020 —

My 16-year-old son has recently been dating a 13-year-old girl. At first, he told me she was 14. Shortly after, I found out she was 13, and I expressed my concerns to him that she is far too young. I hadn't met her yet, but she blocked me from all social media. His behavior has changed. He spends a lot of his time in his room, on his phone, etc. I understand that this can be typical in most new relationships. I have since caught him in a lie/scheme to see her, which included them spending time at a friend's house. I try to keep him as busy as possible. He has lost his phone privileges for some time, and I have since given him a curfew. Recently I have found out that she smokes cigarettes and pot. I also believe she is somewhat sexually active, and to my knowledge, my son is not. I have tried to speak to my son about drug abuse with little to no response. Her father knows of the relationship and the age difference, and my son tells me that her father is not concerned. I am at a loss. Do I contact her father? Do I set further limitations on time spent with her? I am trying to be very careful as the more I push, the more he will want to see her. HELP!

— Confused Mom

 

Dear Confused Mom,

You mention a couple of very different concerns. One is that your son's girlfriend is too young for him, while the other seems to be that she might be a bad influence on him.

On the age point, you are rightly concerned, especially if they are, in fact, sexually active. Many states have so-called "Romeo and Juliet" laws, which specify the acceptable age difference between two minor sexual partners for the one who has reached the age of consent to avoid liability for statutory rape. In some states, the age of consent is 16, and the acceptable age difference is only two years. So if your 16-year-old son is in a sexual relationship with his 13-year old girlfriend, that could be legally problematic depending on where you live. (This is not intended as and should not be considered legal advice. See the note on our homepage (http://askdrm.org/), for more information.)

Legal concerns aside, there is a reason why there is no safe harbor for sexual relationships involving 16 and 13-year olds in some places: the difference in emotional, mental, and physical maturity between a 16-year-old and a 13-year-old can be very significant. That may be the case here, or it may not. Still, if you think that the maturity difference is substantial enough that the relationship is inappropriate, then you have a responsibility to take action. You could put a stop to the relationship, or otherwise take steps to prevent it from continuing, which may include contacting the girl's father and mother or other guardians. While her father is fine with it, that doesn't necessarily mean that others responsible for her wellbeing are on-board.

As for your other concern, that this girl may be a bad influence—assuming that the relationship is not inappropriate, then you may want to tread more carefully. Implementing punishments or taking other actions—like just trying to keep him busy, without tying them to particular infractions—sends a confusing message to your son that he is subject to punishment and adverse treatment irrespective of whether he obeys your rules. Assuming again for the sake of argument, that the relationship is not inappropriate, then it's perfectly normal for him to want to see her. If your rules prevent him from doing that, he will try to get around them, and you should expect as much. Consider well whether or not what you are forbidding is reasonable. Educate your son about the risks of drugs and being sexually active and set consistent rules and consequences regarding such activities. If you think the relationship is inappropriate, then you should end it outright. But if not, then trying to put an end to it indirectly with excessive or arbitrary prohibitions and punishments will likely just result in broken rules and broken trust.
